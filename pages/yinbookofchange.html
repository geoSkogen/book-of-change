what's a hexagram?


As the name implies, a hexagram consists of six lines. These can represent either yin or yang.

yin line: – –
yang line: —

The Chinese of antiquity drew hexagrams from manipulated yarrow stalks. I learned to draw hexagrams by tossing coins: build hexgrams from the bottom–up, each line based on the coin-toss results.


There is a good chance that a coin-toss will draw a "moving" line. Either yin will dissolve into yang, or yang dissolve into yin. This always results in two hexagrams: one with moving lines, and the other with the still lines that follow.


trigrams

The hexagram is actually the joining two trigrams. The set of trigrams contains all eight possible combinations of yin and yang that result from three coin-tosses. Each trigram represents an element or force of nature.



The intersection of two trigrams indentifies one of the 64 possible hexagrams. The hexagram represents a single phase—one 'change'—in an ever-moving stream of changes. A hexagram with moving lines also demands a look at one of the 4032 (63 x 64) possible shifts between phases as represented in one hexagram becoming another.

throwing hexagrams

Click the line-numbered buttons to build your hexagram. Each button draws a randomly generated line. If it's a "moving" line, a second hexagram will appear with the resulting line changes—a yang line becoming yin or the reverse.

Observe how line 3 becomes yang and line 6 becomes yin, generating a new hexagram. This application interprets hexagrams, along with moving lines of the first hexagram.

read

The "read" buttons opens a modal identifying the resulting hexagram(s), and their constituent trigrams.

interpret

Click the hexagram to show its interpretation menu


Hexagrams have both inner and outer properties, and so do their moving or still lines. The inner purports and lines pertain to one's inward life, and the outer to one's social, familial, professional, etc. life. The moving lines of the first hexagram are meditations upon the coming change, the second hexagram.

The Yin Book of Change is an attempt to restore for the contemporary reader a more inclusive ethic and worldview than offered by millenia of patriarchal orientation.   It aims to reinstate the order of the hexagrams handed down from obscure beginnings which predate the rise of patriarchy.


In roughly 1100 BCE, King Wen, father of the founder of the Chou dynasty updated the already ancient I Ching, or Book of Change, reversing the order which, until then, began with Yin.   King Wen placed Yang first, an arrangement the remains in texts to this day.


In the Hebrew Cabala, the female is characterized by severity and the male by mercy.   In our times these characteristics, like the order of Yin and Yang, appear to be reversed.


We have attempted to restore the natural order as in the ancient code, where Yang is merciful and Yin is severe.

the Liebniz connection

Gottfried Liebniz

At the turn of the 18th Century this German philosopher—now cannonized as the man who invented calculus—had also invented an arithmetic we now take for granted as the binary system. Being one of the first European thinkers to take a scholarly interst in Chinese civilization, Liebniz would've already seen how the ancient I Ching's hexagrams corresponded to the binary numbers 000000 through 111111—0 to 63.

Liebniz reveled in the I Ching, citing its mere existence as affirmation of his own religious beliefs. He even sent a copy of the 64 hexagrams to the Duke of Brunswick, enclosed with a letter:

It is not easy to impart the creation ex nihilo through God's almighty power. Now one can say that nothing in the world can better present and demonstrate this power better than the origin of numbers, as it is presented here through the simple unadorned presentation of One and Zero or Nothing.

Despite its centrality to his spiritual convictions, Liebniz's binary math aroused scant interest among thinkers of his era. It was only via happenstance spanning a nearly 140-year succession of scholarship that his system fell into place as the indispensible object code which we still use to communicate with machines.



George Boole

In 1854 he published An Investigation of the Laws of Thought, proposing that logic might proceed through a type of algebra based on binary variables—either true or false, represented as 1 or 0—and the formal logical operators of conjunction, disjunction, negation, implication, exclusivity, and equivalence.


Claude E. Shannon

As part of his 1937 master's thesis at MIT, he included a section demonstrating that any numerical or logical problem could be navigated through the arrangement of electro-mechanical relays using Boolean algebra and binary arithmetic.


John Atanasoff

In 1939, while serving as a physics professor at Iowa State, he prototyped what is now recognized as the first digital computer—an electronic device employing vacuum tubes as switches which solved simultaneous linear equations through binary math and Boolean logic.


Grace Hopper

As part of Harvard University's Bureau of Ships Computation Project, she pioneered programming of the IBM Automatic Sequence Controlled Calculator—the "Mark I"—during World War II. This led to her development of A-0—Arithmetic Language version 0—which translated programmers' source code into binary object code for the newly constructed UNIVAC I. This was the first compiler.


. . . and here we are.
